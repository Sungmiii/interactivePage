AOS officer Damian White tells his story of being shot point blank in the Kawerau siege

by Carmet Parahi

Armed Offenders Squad

Damian White was one of four AOS officers shot by Rhys Warren near Kawerau three years ago. He was not only injured in the shooting but also deeply wounded by the criticism aimed at him and his fellow officers by other cops, the public and Māori community. White's putting his hardman reputation on the line to let others know it's okay to feel hurt and show it. National Correspondent Carmen Parahi reports.

written by CARMEN PARAHI

At every armed call out, Damian White carried a high powered rifle in his hands and a Glock pistol strapped to one thigh.

Swathed in black from head to toe, he wore a ballistic vest to protect him from being stabbed, slashed or shot at. Over the top was his equipment vest that held pepper spray, a baton, tear gas and other distraction devices.

During his AOS career, White confronted and arrested hundreds of armed offenders throughout the Bay of Plenty. He sat, gun ready, for hours in the house next door to Jan Molenaar, the Napier gunman who killed Senior Constable Len Snee. In the Solomon Islands, he helped contain village warfare, led the recovery of an Australian cop who fell to his death in a cave and tracked killers through the bush.

Remarkably, not once did he fire his gun. Not a single bullet.

"There's been ample jobs I've been on where there would have been the right to shoot someone and take a life," says White.

"But you don't want to, that's the last thing you want to do. No police officer wants to take a life."

White is a proud Māori cop of Te Arawa descent. A grandfather of one moko, with another grandchild on the way. He has two adult children, a teenage stepson and young daughter who worry about his safety. His wife is also concerned for him, about how the public and other police will react to her husband's story.

White spent 16 years in the blue uniform, 14 kitted in black AOS gear. He was based in the Bay of Plenty, the land of his ancestors. He'd still be a cop now but three years ago, his life changed.

THE RACE CARD

During the standoff between police and Warren, his whānau gathered at a cordon talking to the news media accusing police of over-reacting because Warren was Māori.

White has shared ancestry with Warren, their grandmothers also played golf together. White's mum was angry about other Māori playing the race card against her son.

"It's what really got me down," says White.

"I'd go to work everyday trying to do the best I could for Māori and every cop does, it's not just me, even the non-sworn staff they're there trying to do their best.

"It's not quite soul destroying but it's a really horrible thing to deal with," he says with emotion in his voice.

I'm proud of being Māori. I don't know any Māori cop that's not proud of being Māori. We're out there trying to make a difference for our communities. It's an easy beat up because we don't have the right of reply.
